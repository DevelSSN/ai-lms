\chapter{Literature Survey}

The educational landscape has undergone a significant transformation over recent decades, transitioning from traditional physical instruction, the large-scale delivery of Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs) to the current AI era. Despite the increased accessibility of online learning platforms, providing instruction tailored to individual student needs remains a challenge—a manifestation widely characterized as the \enquote{Two Sigma Problem} \cite{bloom1984}. Conventional digital platforms often rely on video instruction similar to a classroom, which lack the personalization required to emulate one-to-one tutoring. 

However, the integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Large Language Models (LLMs) has created new opportunities to approximate the benchmarks defined by \textcite{bloom1984}. While LLMs offer promising capabilities in simulating intelligent dialogue, current implementations showcase notable limitations in adaptive behavior. This literature survey identifies and analyzes these research gaps, assessing the state of AI-driven educational frameworks.

\section{Overview}
This literature survey explores the current horizon of artificial intelligence in education, passing thorugh from a static tool to modern dynamic support systems. Systematic reviews by \textcite{chen2020}, \textcite{elbasi2025} and \textcite{agbewali2022} provide a foundational taxonomy of machine learning and deep learning applications, while \textcite{mallik2023} categorizes these into proactive methods, such as content planning, and reactive methods, including performance assessment. 

The field is currently moving away from simple content delivery toward intelligent interaction. We see a transition where platforms no longer just host media but actively monitor student behavior to intervene before a learner loses interest. This shift is particularly visible in technical domains like Digital Signal Processing (DSP), where pedagogy has evolved from mathematical theory to interactive, simulation-based, and now AI-enhanced learning \cite{lopez2025, boldo2024}.

In this literature survey, we filtered research studies using subsets of the following keywords: artificial intelligence in education; machine learning; deep reinforcement learning; student engagement; personalized learning; digital signal processing; education; pedagogy; teaching methods; Active learning (AL), edge AI, edge training, online distillation,

\section{Detailed Survey}

The literature survey reveals four primary pillars of AI integration:

\begin{enumerate}
	\item Multi-Agent Systems and autonomy: \\
	Traditional e-learning often lacks human intuition. Recent work has focused on Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) to provide dynamic adaptation. \textcite{zakaria2025} explains how a multi-agent module (MAT) designed using the Markovian process as the differentiator between different modules. \textcite{vukovic2021} demonstrates a passive observer that studies the activities of a user. % Further advancement using the Von Neumann framework proposed by \textcite{jiang2024} suggest a shift toward fully autonomous environments where AI agents manage personalized learning pathways and manage the orchestration of complex educational tasks.
	
	\item Predictive Analytics and Affective Computing: \\
	The ability to foresee where a student may struggle is actively researched upon in the field of educational A. \textcite{bagunaid2024} utilizes deep reinforcement learning model to create early warning systems for student performance, while \textcite{rizwan2025} identifies factors affecting student performance in MOOCs through systematic review. Beyond academic scores, \textcite{hasan2020} emphasizes the transition toward affective tutoring systems, which incorporate emotional intelligence and student engagement levels \cite{ayouni2021} into the learning loop. These systems, while computationally expensive to run, provide a framework upon which to build emotionally aware LLMs. 
	\textcite{mahafdah2024} uses the data generated through current LMS systems to produce student insights by utilizing ML models like Random Forest, XGBoost, and RNN among many others. \textcite{aderghal2025} demonstrates that deep learning architectures, specifically CNNs and xLSTM for knowledge tracing, significantly outperform traditional statistical models in capturing sequential learning patterns.
	
	\item Large Language Models and Cognitive Diagnosis: \\
	LLMs have introduced advanced reasoning capabilities to education. \textcite{zhang2025} introduces the SPP-L² framework for performance prediction on learner-sourced questions, using a mixture of graphs, LLMs and RL models to make feedback reward based, producing better results than its peers. \textcite{chen2025} explores LLM-enhanced cognitive diagnosis for deeper student understanding, creating a Q-matrix from the hidden information the LLM (Llama3-70B) extracts in the user prompts. Other practical applications include specialized tutoring tools like PETRA \cite{hewedy2025}, which provides recursive assistance for dynamic programming; proactive UI agents for visual analytics \cite{zhao2025} and MAT, a multi-agent tutoring module \cite{zakaria2025}. These systems move beyond simple text generation to active, concept-centric evaluation and remedial recommendations \cite{pradeesh2025}.
	
	\item Immersive Learning and the Edu-Metaverse: \\
	The spatial dimension of digital education is being redefined through virtual environments. \textcite{illi2025} and \textcite{teichmann2025} provide frameworks for the Edu-Metaverse, designing immersive virtual learning based on real-world processes. These environments aim to increase engagement by providing high-fidelity simulations. Other research papers measure the effectiveness of AI in research-based learning models \cite{festiyed2026} and big data \cite{bakhouyi2025}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Research Gap Identification}

Despite these technological strides, several critical gaps remain:

\begin{itemize}
	\item Concept Drift and Memory Decay: \textcite{hajmohammed2025} highlights that LLMs suffer from concept drift as language and educational contexts evolve. Furthermore, most current systems stand at risk of fragmented learning experiences due to lack of a persistent, long-term memory of student progress across different learning sessions.
	\item Multimodal Knowledge Extraction: While AI can process text, there is a lack of integration that can autonomously convert diverse raw media, such as instructional videos and complex technical documents, into structured, evaluative knowledge concepts \cite{pradeesh2025, lopez2025}.
	\item Fragmented Feedback Mechanisms: Current reactive systems often provide generic grading \cite{mallik2023} rather than personalized reports based on a specific user persona and historical progress \cite{hasan2020}.
	\item Scalability of Agentic Systems: While deep reinforcement learning is powerful for multi-agent systems, \textcite{nguyen2020} notes that training stable agents in dynamic educational settings remains a significant challenge, often requiring high computational overhead that is difficult to scale for individual users \cite{boldo2024}.
\end{itemize}
